Why full body instead of upper/lower: At only 2 gym sessions per week, a split means each muscle group gets trained once per week — suboptimal for hypertrophy. Full body means every major muscle is stimulated twice per week, which research consistently shows produces significantly more muscle growth at the same total volume.

Why 6–7 exercises maximum: More exercises means more fatigue and reduced recovery for Tuesday kickboxing and Sunday boxing. Every exercise here earns its place. Simple, repeatable, recovery-safe for 8–12 weeks.

What each session targets: Wednesday is your strength day — heavier loads, lower reps, built around the trap bar deadlift. This is where your nervous system learns to produce maximum force. Friday is your volume day — moderate reps, more total work, built around the squat. This is where most muscle size gets built. Together they cover every pattern twice a week without repeating the same lift.

Why neck and forearms get direct work, not biceps: Every exercise here is chosen for transfer, not looks. A stronger neck resists the head-snap that causes a KO — structural protection, not vanity. Forearms determine grip, clinch control, and wrist stability on impact. Biceps already get plenty of indirect work from pull-ups and rows — isolating them further adds fatigue without adding fight performance.

05 — Mon & Sat

Fighter Mobility System

Kick Height = Mobility + End Range Strength + Balance + Hip Control

Why active flexibility matters beyond fighting: Range you can hold under your own strength keeps joints healthy long-term — most people lose mobility not from tight tissue, but from never loading the end range, so the nervous system shuts it down.

Stretching alone does NOT create head kicks. You need active flexibility — the ability to hold your kick at height, not just passively reach it.

08 — Guidelines

Keep It Simple

  • —Never fail a rep on compound lifts — leave 1–2 in the tank always
  • —Explosive finishers are optional — skip if combat sessions left you fatigued
  • —Neck training capped at 2 exercises per week — more creates tightness that hurts striking
  • —Friday finish by evening — you need Saturday to recover before Sunday boxing
  • —Don't add extra gym days — your combat classes already tax your recovery
  • —Months 1–2: learn the movements at moderate weight. Month 3+: push hard
  • —Track every lift — write the weight and reps down after every set
  • —Gain weight slowly — 0.25kg/week max keeps it lean, not fat

If you miss a session: Just resume the normal rhythm next week — don't double up or stack workouts to "catch up." One missed session costs almost nothing over 16–18 months. If weight or reps slip, simply resume at your last logged number.

Every 8–12 weeks, reassess: Take a deload week at ~60% weight, normal reps. Retest your top lifts over 1–2 sessions to find new working weights, then restart the progression model. Use this checkpoint to review bodyweight progress and swap out anything causing joint pain or a stuck plateau.

09 — Purpose

What This Program Is & Isn't

This is not a bodybuilding program. It is not a powerlifting program. It is a combat athlete full-body strength program — designed for someone who already trains kickboxing and boxing twice a week and needs the gym to complement, not compete with, those sessions.

The objective: add lean muscle mass and maximal strength to a long-limbed, 61kg combat athlete without impairing striking performance, speed, or recovery.

What it does
  • Trains every muscle group twice per week
  • Builds total-body strength through compound lifts
  • Adds functional muscle mass to long levers
  • Develops structural armour — neck, traps, forearms
  • Trains explosive power without conditioning fatigue
  • Protects shoulders, spine, and joints long-term
What it doesn't do
  • Chase hypertrophy at the cost of recovery
  • Add volume that bleeds into combat days
  • Prioritise aesthetics over athletic function
  • Include conditioning that competes with boxing
  • Train to failure on heavy lifts
  • Stack posterior chain fatigue session-over-session
10 — Physics & Science

Why Size Matters in Combat

Size in combat sports is not vanity — it is physics. Every principle below is backed by classical mechanics.

01
Force = Mass × Acceleration
Newton's Second Law
F = m × a

A punch is a force. If you throw a cross at the same speed but weigh 76kg instead of 61kg, the force at the end of that fist is proportionally greater. More muscle fibres recruited = higher acceleration AND higher effective mass — both increase simultaneously. This is why heavier fighters hit harder even at identical technique.

02
Kinetic Energy
The Energy in Every Strike
KE = ½ × m × v²

Velocity is squared — speed matters enormously, which is why you must not sacrifice it for size. But mass still multiplies everything. A punch at 10m/s with 5kg effective mass carries 250 joules; with 6kg it carries 300 joules — a 20% increase in destructive energy for a 20% increase in mass.

03
Momentum
Why Bigger Fighters Are Harder to Stop
p = m × v

At 61kg, even moving aggressively, a 75kg opponent at the same speed carries 23% more momentum. Your target of 74–77kg levels that equation entirely. Adding 13–16kg of lean mass transforms your clinch control and ability to absorb and redirect incoming force.

04
Impulse & Impact Force
How Hard You Can Take a Shot
F = Δp / Δt

A thick, strong neck increases Δt — slowing the rate of head movement by absorbing energy in muscle tissue rather than transmitting it instantly to the brain. Same principle as a crumple zone on a car. Lean mass also increases your body's total inertia, making you harder to move, hurt, and finish.

05
Rotational Power & Torque
Where Punching Power Really Comes From
τ = r × F

Every punch is a rotational movement. With your long arms (82cm) and legs (106cm), your natural radius is exceptionally large. Even modest force increases from stronger glutes and hips produce disproportionately large torque at the end of your limbs. Your body type is an advantage — this program weaponises it.

06
Your Sweet Spot
Size Without Losing Speed
74–77kg

Lean muscle does NOT slow you down. Excess body fat does. Strength training improves Rate of Force Development — muscles fire faster, not slower. At 181cm, 74–77kg is your optimal power-to-weight band.

  • Strength training improves RFD — muscles fire faster, not slower
  • Lean mass at 0.25kg/week adds almost no adipose tissue
  • Stronger glutes and hips = more explosive hip rotation = faster punches

Past 80kg the math flips. Muscle is metabolically expensive — more tissue means more oxygen demand, but your heart's stroke volume stays fixed. You end up diluting oxygen delivery rather than improving it, so you gas faster and slow down in later rounds. 74–77kg is the ceiling where added mass still pays for itself.

07
The Neck — KO Shield
Why Neck Thickness Saves You
α = τ / I

A KO happens when the brain rotates rapidly inside the skull. A thick neck dramatically increases moment of inertia — so the same punch produces less head rotation, less brain movement, less shearing on neural tissue. Fighters with stronger necks absorb significantly higher impact before cognitive disruption.

08
Thick Forearms — Weapons & Shields
Stress Distribution · Impact Absorption
σ = F / A

More forearm cross-section = less stress per unit of tissue = safer, more rigid striking platform. Thick forearms also absorb incoming blocks — in kickboxing, your forearm IS the shield. Grip strength from thick forearms dominates clinch exchanges and prevents wrist injuries.

09
Why It Matters for Future MMA
Leverage & The Long-Limb Problem
τ = r × F

The same long limbs that win you reach on the feet work against you on the mat by default — torque equals lever length times force, so an opponent gripping your wrist or ankle has a longer lever to control, and at 61kg you currently lack the force to resist it. This program closes that gap early: trap bar deadlifts, pull-ups, and farmer's carries build the grip and full-body tension needed to resist control at range, so your reach becomes an advantage in both phases once grappling enters the picture.
